## Problem

Commuters in Sri Lanka rely on public buses with no reliable way to know where a bus actually is, what a fare will cost, or what to do about a delayed route or a lost bag. TrackNGo set out to fix that with real-time GPS tracking fed by conductors, fare estimation, and a lost-and-found system — as a five-person team project split across five repositories (rider backend/frontend, admin backend/frontend, and shared docs), which meant the real engineering problem wasn't just "build the features," it was coordinating five people across two full-stack surfaces without the codebases drifting apart.

## Architecture / Approach

- **Two parallel Express/MongoDB APIs** — a core backend serving riders and conductors (GPS updates, route/fare lookups, lost-and-found) and a separate admin backend serving the operations dashboard (conductor accounts, contact-form triage, moderation) — kept as separate services so admin tooling changes don't risk the rider-facing API.
- **Socket.io** pushes live bus location updates from conductor devices to rider apps without polling.
- **React frontends** (rider + admin, also split into separate repos) consume these APIs independently, each with its own deploy lifecycle.
- **Mongoose schemas** model buses, routes, reports, emergencies, and form submissions, with JWT securing conductor- and admin-only endpoints.

## My Contribution

On the **admin backend**, I was the primary contributor: I built the contact/inquiry system end to end (model, controller, routes for bus-service and technical submissions), a conductor password-change flow, and the form-submission pipeline with typed submission statuses. On the **core backend**, I built the incident-reporting system (create/delete/comment on rider reports, with admin comment threads), the emergency-alert feature for conductors to flag a bus in distress, a location-based fare-estimate search, and a news-feed integration that pulls and stores bus-related articles from an external API. Working across two live backends with four other contributors meant every route needed to ship without breaking the GPS/tracking path the rest of the team depended on — most of my PRs were reviewed against "does this touch the live-tracking flow" before anything else.

## Outcome

TrackNGo shipped as a working five-repo platform: live tracking, fare estimation, lost-and-found, incident reporting, emergency alerts, and an admin operations dashboard, built and merged across a five-person team without the rider- and admin-facing systems colliding. It's the project that taught me the most about coordinating backend ownership across a team — splitting by resource (reports vs. emergencies vs. admin submissions) rather than by person kept merge conflicts rare even with five people committing to overlapping codebases.
