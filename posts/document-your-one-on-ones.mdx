Early in my internship, one-on-ones with my supervisor felt like weather: they happened, they were useful in the moment, and then they evaporated. A month later I could remember the mood of a conversation but not its contents. Which is a problem, because those conversations contained the highest-density feedback I was getting, and I was storing it in the least reliable medium available.

The habit that fixed it is minimal: within an hour after each one-on-one, five minutes writing three things into a dated note. What was discussed, in a few bullets. What I committed to, verbatim as commitments ("investigate X by Friday," not "talked about X"). And any feedback, praise or correction, in something close to the actual words used.

Each of the three earns its place differently.

The discussion bullets are for continuity. Next meeting, thirty seconds of review means I arrive knowing what's open, what I said last time, and what they said, which converts meetings from disconnected episodes into a running conversation. It also quietly signals that things said to me don't disappear, and people give better feedback to someone who visibly retains it.

The commitments are for trust. Small asks mentioned in passing ("could you also look at...") are precisely the ones that slip, and a slipped small promise costs more credibility than its size justifies. Written down, they migrate into my daily task list and actually happen.

The feedback log is for the long game, and it's the one I'd defend hardest. Feedback arrives as scattered single sentences across months. Individually they're easy to nod at and forget. Collected, they show patterns: the same theme mentioned twice is a real growth area, not an offhand comment. When my internship's monthly reflection reports come due, this log is the difference between reconstructing my development from memory and quoting it with dates. And on rough weeks, rereading the accumulated positive notes is a surprisingly effective antidote to impostor feelings, because it's evidence rather than reassurance.

Two boundaries keep the habit healthy. The notes are for me, private by default, which keeps them honest. And they're written after the meeting, not during; being present beats being a stenographer, and the recall gap over one hour is small.

None of this is sophisticated. It's the workplace version of writing down what the teacher said. I'm just no longer embarrassed that it took me weeks to start.
