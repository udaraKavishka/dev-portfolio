Most autoscaling watches CPU utilization, which is a lagging indicator by definition: by the time CPU is high, the load already arrived. If you're trying to act earlier, the interesting question is which metrics lead. From time spent staring at cluster dashboards, a few candidates consistently carry information about the near future.

Queue depth is the classic. Whatever your system's queue is (message broker backlog, CI jobs pending, task queue length), a growing queue is future CPU spelled out explicitly. The work exists, it's enumerated, it just hasn't executed yet. If pending-jobs jumps by fifty, worker demand in the next minutes is not a forecast, it's arithmetic. Any scaler ignoring queue metrics in favor of CPU is refusing free information.

Connection and session counts lead the resource cost of what those connections will do. New WebSocket connections, rising active sessions, login rate: each precedes the requests those users are about to generate. The lead time is short, seconds to a minute, but pod startup takes about that long, so it's exactly the lead you can use.

Rate of change beats level. Two services at 50% CPU are not equivalent when one has been flat for an hour and the other climbed from 20% in two minutes. The derivative carries the urgency. PromQL's `deriv()` or a `rate()` over a short window turns your existing gauges into leading-ish indicators without collecting anything new.

The calendar is a metric too, and it embarrasses me how long I took to treat it as one. Cron schedules, business hours, release-day rhythms: these are the strongest predictors most clusters have, and they're literally written down. Hour-of-day and day-of-week explain a large share of variance in any workload touched by humans. A model, or even a static schedule, that knows Monday 9am from Sunday 3am starts with an advantage no amount of CPU-watching provides.

Upstream volume, finally: your service's future load is your caller's current load. If the frontend tier is spiking now, the API tier's spike is in transit. Cross-service correlation with a lag is crude to exploit by hand but real.

The general test for any candidate: plot it against CPU shifted several minutes into the future and check the correlation. Strong correlation at a positive lag means the metric knows something early, and anything that knows something early is worth feeding to whatever makes your scaling decisions, whether that's a trained model or a human writing threshold rules.
