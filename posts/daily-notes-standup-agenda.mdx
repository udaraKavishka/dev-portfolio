Standup used to be a small daily memory test I kept failing. What did I do yesterday? Something with the invoice bug. Also a review? By the time my turn came I'd reconstructed maybe 70% of my own day, in the wrong order, missing the part that actually mattered.

The fix I stumbled into is embarrassingly simple: one note per day, kept in my notes app, structured as checklists with links to the issues I'm touching. Yesterday's items that survived migrate to today. When standup arrives, my update is already written; I read the note instead of excavating my memory. Standup became the moment the note is consumed rather than an event I prepare for.

What makes it stick, I think, is that the note isn't ceremony. It's a working document with three jobs that happen to share a file.

It's a plan: the morning's five minutes writing today's checklist is really a prioritization pass wearing a to-do list costume. Deciding what makes the list, and what leads it, is the decision that shapes the day, and doing it in writing beats doing it by vibes at 9am.

It's a log: items checked off with links to the PRs and issues they produced. This pays compound interest at month's end when I write reports (my internship requires a monthly reflection journal, and assembling it from thirty daily notes takes an evening instead of a weekend of archaeology). It also answers the occasional "wait, when did we change that?" with a date and a link.

It's a parking lot: mid-task interruptions ("can you also look at...") get one line in the note instead of an immediate context switch. The request is safe, my focus survives, and tomorrow-me triages it with a clear head. Cross-cutting reminders that outlive a single day get promoted to a separate running list, so the daily note stays disposable.

The mechanics barely matter. Mine lives in Obsidian because linking to issue notes is easy there, but a text file named by date does 90% of it. The two rules that do matter: write it the same morning ritual every day, so it never depends on motivation, and keep it small enough that maintaining it costs less than the confusion it prevents. Mine takes five minutes a day. The old standup fumbling cost more than that in dignity alone.
